A brief look at the salty winter in the New York City

by Hongyu Liu


New York City welcomed its first snow of the season earlier this week. Besides the joyful sled ride down the slope and the struggle to dodge a puddle when walking through a crosswalk, a snowy New York City is not complete without stepping on the crunchy road salt.


Road salt lower the freezing point of water, hence preventing ice formation and making the road safer for drivers and pedestrians. The most common form of road salt is the mineral sodium chloride. Table salt is a purified form of this mineral salt.


As early as 1938, New Hampshire began using granit sodium chloride to deice its road networks.


Now after decades of use, road salt have proved to have multiple drawbacks that are difficult to look over.


In 2014, the USGS, for example, attributed the use of road salt as the primary cause for the increasing chloride concentration in urban streams across the nation. Over one-forth of the sites they studied exceeded the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency’s chronic water-quality criteria (230 milligrams per liter) by an average of more than 100 days per year from 2006 through 2011, double the amount of days from 1990 through 1994. Further more, the study found an increase in chloride levels in summer as well, suggests that chloride infiltrates the groundwater system during the winter and is slowly released to the streams throughout the year.


In 2020, the New York State legislature created a pilot program to reduce road salt use in the Adirondacks after local wells exceeded federal sodium limits. The program also pointed out that road salt can cause rust to vehicles and infrastructure.

So, what about NYC?


There is no adequate data to measure the direct impact of road salt on the natural water resources in New York City during the past decade.


The city’s Department of Sanitation is the main road salt deployer in the five boroughs. They deployed over 300,000 tons of road salt during the winter of 2021 - 2022.


As shown by the chart below, the DSNY deploys more salt when there is heavier snowfall in the city. However, the agency on average deploys more tons of salt for every inch of snow when the snowfall is lighter.

Ashly Smith, a physical scientist at the USGS New York Water Science Center, said snowmelt, precipitation, and runoff can bring high concentrations of salt into the river.


However, the salinity of the two main rivers running through the city, the Hudson River and East River, fluctuates as the salty Atlantic Ocean water flux into the river mouth during high tides.


Bronx River is the only major watercourse in the city that is not entirely tidal, making it an ideal target to study the road salt run-off in the NYC area. Since June 2021, USGS expanded their range of monitoring items at their New York Botanical Garden site where the Bronx River flows through, including the water salinity.


As shown by the chart below, the salinity of the Bronx River at this site remains stable at about 0.5 PSU year-round. In the winter from 2021-2022, days after over six inches of snow piled in the city, the river salinity peaked at 2.8 PSU, a number still lower than the 5 PSU salinity threshold for the low-salt area.
